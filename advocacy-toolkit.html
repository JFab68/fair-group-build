Understanding the Arizona Legislative Process

How a bill becomes law in Arizona, key timelines, and where advocates can intervene.

2027 Session Timeline

January

Session convenes. Bill introductions begin. Committee hearings start. Advocates: For current-session bills, activate RTS accounts and schedule legislator meetings to support bills already in play. For next session, begin asking legislators and stakeholders what gaps this session left open and what ideas they will want to carry forward.

February

Committee hearings continue. Bills moving through first committees. Advocates: For current-session bills, testify at hearings and mobilize constituents in key districts to keep bills alive. For next session, watch which bills make it through committee and which die, and use that to identify what policy areas the coalition should develop into new bills next year.

March

Bill hearings intensify. Floor debates begin in originating chamber. Advocates: For current-session bills, intensify floor vote persuasion in the chamber where those bills are active. For next session, watch how votes break, which amendments succeed, and which coalitions form, then power map the players, relationships, and cleavage points that will shape those same fights next year.

April

Critical Deadline: Bills must pass originating chamber by end of month or die. Crossover to second chamber begins. Advocates: For current-session bills, mount all-hands mobilization for crossover bills. For next session, track which members and committees exercised power and on what terms, so the coalition can plan smarter targeting and sequencing for the following year.

May

Final votes and budget negotiations. Conference committees resolve differences. Advocates: For current-session bills, pressure leadership and conference committee members on final language. For next session, document what concessions were won and lost, who was aligned or opposed, and use that intelligence to build sponsor pledges and bill concepts for the following year.

June-July

Governor review period (5-day regular veto, 10-day after session adjournment). Override requires 2/3 vote. Advocates: Direct advocacy to Governor's office on pending bills.

August

Summer recess closes. Implementation work begins for passed bills. Advocates: Track agency rulemaking and program implementation; identify gaps that may require follow-up legislation.

September

Interim committee work resumes. Advocates: Submit testimony, identify champions for 2028 session, and build relationships with newly assigned committee members.

October

Strategic planning period. Advocates: Finalize 2028 legislative priorities, refine policy language, and coordinate with coalition partners on bill sponsorship outreach.

November

Election cycle influences surface. Advocates: Engage with transition teams, analyze election results for new legislative makeup, and begin 2028 session scheduling.

December

Year-end coalition review. Advocates: Conduct lessons-learned analysis, update strategic documents, recognize member achievements, and confirm 2028 legislative interventions.

Strategic Pre-File Workflow

This period is about shaping ideas before they become legislation. Use it to make the session more efficient and better informed.

June

Review the prior session and assess which bills moved, which failed, and why. By this point bill ideas for the next session should be semi-formalized and sketched out so advocates can clearly describe the problem, proposed solution, and target outcome. Hold a meeting to compare ideas, decide priorities, and identify sponsors.

Once sponsors are identified and have signed on, send bill ideas to Legislative Council by mid-to-late July so concepts can be drafted into legislative proposals.

July

Legislative Council converts approved bill ideas into legislative proposals.

August

Preliminary stakeholder education. Expect limited availability; use this month for initial outreach and scheduling rather than expecting full attendance.

September-October

Convene targeted stakeholder meetings around specific bills. Hear from opposition, supporting advocates, and neutral parties. Use that feedback to revise and finalize working draft language.

December

File or prefile legislation with the sponsor so the bill receives a bill number. Having bill language ready early gives advocates a concrete, rally-ready vehicle heading into session.

Key Principles for Drafting Arizona Legislation

1

Read similar bills first. Find legislation on related topics in Arizona and other states. Understand what has already been tried and what language courts have interpreted.

2

Keep it simple. Avoid wordiness and unnecessary complexity. Every word will be read by courts, agencies, and litigants. Clarity prevents unintended consequences.

3

Precision counts. "Shall" creates mandates; "may" creates permissions. "That" introduces restrictive clauses; "which" introduces non-restrictive ones. See the Bill Drafting Manual.

4

Have experts review. Before introducing, have attorneys, legislative counsel, and subject-matter experts review the draft. FAMM offers legislative examples upon request.

Request to Speak (RTS) System

Arizona's remote advocacy platform. How to register, speak at hearings, and amplify your voice.

Getting Started with RTS

Step 1: Activate Your Account

You must physically visit the Capitol once to create an RTS account at a kiosk (House, Senate, or Tucson office). After activation, all advocacy can be done remotely.

Step 2: Register Your Opinion

Log in to RTS to register support or opposition on bills. Leave comments for committee members explaining your position with constituent impact details.

Step 3: Request to Speak

Request to speak at committee hearings. You can testify in person or remotely. The system tracks requests and committee chairs call speakers in order.

Official RTS System Guide

Government

Official state guide to using the Request to Speak system. Explains the one-time physical activation requirement, how to register opinions, leave comments for committee members, and request to speak at hearings. Critical tool for remote advocacy.
